## The Quillate Consensus (player-facing)

### The world is a planet

The learned consensus, from star observation and horizon geometry: the
Veilwrack wraps a **large, deep-aired world** circling an ordinary sun. The
sun rises and sets; there are seasons (the High Gales, the Wet, the Long
Calm, the Mourning Fogs); there is a moon, small and fast, useful for night
navigation. Gravity is real and constant — slightly gentler, the Quillate's
pendulum-keepers insist, than "bone-weight reckoning" would predict, which
is part of why flight as large as an Alar is possible at all.

The habitable band is a matter of **air pressure and temperature**. Go too
high and the air thins and chills past use; descend and it thickens, warms,
and grows heavy until — at the Undermist — it crosses into something that
behaves as much like a sea as a sky. There is, presumably, a *bottom* far
below: rock or ocean under pressures no bone could bear. No one has seen it.
The world's surface, if it has one, is as theoretical to the Alar as the
back of the sky.

### Why the Spires float

No mysticism required: **buoyancy**. Spire bone is a rigid foam — millions
of sealed cells of pale ossein, each holding **marrow-breath**, a gas
lighter than air (the Lanternfall lamp-masters, who burn it daily, will tell
you it burns the way swamp-vapors burn, hot and clean). A Spire is, in
effect, a rigid airship grown by a living thing: the Anemoi were creatures
of buoyant bone, and their husks remain buoyant after death exactly as a
ship remains afloat after its crew dies.

This is also why Spires **sink**: the bone-cells seep, slowly by nature,
quickly when tapped. Lift lost is lift gone; the foam does not refill
itself. (The Marrower Combine's pamphlets say otherwise. The Combine's
pamphlets also say tapping is painless to property values.)

The Undermist's upper surface marks the altitude where the outside air grows
dense enough to carry even seep-weakened bone — which is why drowned spires
like Greywake do not fall forever but settle, wallowing, half-submerged in
the cloud-sea.

### What the Undermist is

The consensus: a **permanent global cloud-deck of dense, hot, wet air** —
the boundary layer where the deep atmosphere's heat meets the habitable
band's cool. Golden by day (dust and droplets in sunlight), green-glowing by
night (the Quillate says luminous microlife, the way some seas glow; the
Ossuin say nothing). It is survivable at the skim with preparation — heat,
pressure, and blindness being the killers, along with predators adapted to
swim-fly in air thick enough to swim in. Deeper than the skim, pressure and
temperature climb without mercy.

Mist-pearls — the Ossuin's condensed droplets — are, chemically, just
Undermist water with dissolved minerals and a faint persistent luminescence.
What unsettles people is not their chemistry.

### What the wind is — the one miracle

Here ordinary meteorology runs out, and honest scholarship says so plainly.

A planet's winds should arise from sunlight and spin: belts, cells, storms —
statistical weather. The Veilwrack *has* such winds. But riding on them is
something else: the **Breath** — the reliable, scheduled, almost-mannered
component of the wind. The windlanes run like rivers in the sky, decade
after decade. Thermals hold longer than heating explains.

The Quillate's best model, the **Vital Circulation Hypothesis**, is
accepted by most and spoken aloud by few, because of where it leads: the
Breath is **biological**. The Anemoi were not riders of the wind but its
*organs* — vast aerial filter-feeders whose metabolism (gas exchange on a
weather-front scale, heat shed in slow pulses) drove and regularized the
circulation of the entire habitable band, the way a forest makes its own
rain. A sky with Anemoi in it was a *tended* sky.

The hypothesis explains, with uncomfortable economy:

1. why the lanes are scheduled (they are the fossil circulation of vanished
   metabolism, sustained by stored heat and the residual breath of the
   husks);
2. why the Breath weakens smoothly century over century (stored anything
   runs down);
3. why Windworking works (Alar lungs and the Breath share one chemistry —
   the working is a conversation between living airs, which is why it costs
   the worker and fails in dead air);
4. and what a Stilling is: **not an invasion but an exhaustion** — a patch
   of sky where the living component of the air has finally run out,
   leaving only dead meteorology: thick, sluggish, sound-swallowing calm.

The hypothesis does not explain — and its authors decline in writing to
speculate — **where all the Anemoi went**, why a sky once full of its own
gardeners emptied, or why the emptying coincides so neatly with the
beginning of the Record. The hypothesis's final page is famous in the deep
stacks for its last sentence: *"The reader is invited to consult the
historical record; the author has, and prefers meteorology."*

### Small consistencies (for the table)

- **Sound in a Stilling:** dead-air zones are temperature-inverted and
  dense-layered; sound refracts down and away. Voices at arm's length,
  silence at ten paces. (And yes — it is worse than the physics strictly
  requires. The physics is doing its best.)
- **Fire:** burns orange and well in the Reach, low and blue in still or
  Root-Dark air (different gas mix, poorer convection). Lantern color is
  every traveler's first still-gauge.
- **Water:** rain falls from the Shade's cloud-streets; spires harvest it
  in catch-sails and bone cisterns. Nobody drinks the Undermist.
- **Food:** skyweed (aerial algae mats), drift-krill (gnat-sized gleaning
  swarms), roost-fowl, netted gliders-fish from the Shade. Brightgyre's
  terraces feed the world; a closed sky is a hungry sky.
- **Metal:** scarce and meteoric. "Storm-iron" is gathered from strike-fused
  reef-bone after lightning storms; most tools are bone, horn, lacquer, and
  woven sinew. A steel knife is an heirloom. (This is why the harpoons of
  legend were *engineered*, not forged — and why the Roak engineered them.)
