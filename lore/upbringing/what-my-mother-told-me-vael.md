## What My Mother Told Me (a Vael childhood, the Moult)

My mother told me this on the high tether, feet hooked in the same rope,
because Vael truths are told while holding on to something.

**Where the world came from.** In the beginning the sky was a herd, little
wing. The great ones — the Anemoi, the grandmother-winds — ran their circuits
and the air ran after them, the way dust runs after the flock. Everything
that flies learned flying from following them. We were the *followers*: first
behind the herd, gleaning, riding the warm wake. When the herd lay down — and
herds do lie down, all herds, this is the first sad thing you learn — they
left us their bones to stand on and their last running still going round and
round the world. The windlanes are hoofprints, child. We live in the
hoofprints of the dead.

**Why we die.** Because we are *small*, and small things are quick, and quick
is paid for. The great ones lived ten thousand years and turned slow as
seasons. We get sixty, and we turn inside a wingspan. Fair trade. When you
die the Ossuin carry you down — let them, pay them, don't haggle — because a
body is ballast and a soul is lift, and what's left of you wants to go *down*
to where the new winds are made, the way rain goes down to come back as
weather. Anyone who tells you the dead are gone has never flown a lane their
grandmother charted.

**Why we are here.** To *carry*, little wing. Word, cargo, kin, fire,
medicine — the world is alive exactly as long as things move through it.
Still air is dead air; a still person is a dead person with the paperwork
pending. You were not born to hold ground. There is no ground.

**The others.** The Roak? Respect them, charge them double. They think the
wind is writing and want to read it; the wind is *running* and wants to be
run with — but a Roak will mend your glider truer than your own sister, and
their word on paper holds when wingbeats fail. The Ossuin — hush, lower your
voice the way I am doing. We owe them. Don't ask what. When a priest of the
Stair asks you for passage, you fly them free, you fly them first, and you
do not ask them questions over open sky. The Crown-folk and their Spirarchy
are landlords of a thing nobody can own; smile, pay the toll, remember the
lane was ours before their grandfathers learned to count it. And the Hushed —
my mother stopped the rope-work when I asked, the only time she stopped —
*the Hushed are what happens when the world forgets to keep moving. Pity
them on paper. Flee them in person.*
