# UPBRINGINGS
### What you were told before you could fly

*Four childhood catechisms — one per background. Give your player the one
that matches their character and let them keep it. Everything in these pages
is what people **believe**. Some of it is even true.*

---

## What My Mother Told Me (a Vael childhood, the Moult)

My mother told me this on the high tether, feet hooked in the same rope,
because Vael truths are told while holding on to something.

**Where the world came from.** In the beginning the sky was a herd, little
wing. The great ones — the Anemoi, the grandmother-winds — ran their circuits
and the air ran after them, the way dust runs after the flock. Everything
that flies learned flying from following them. We were the *followers*: first
behind the herd, gleaning, riding the warm wake. When the herd lay down — and
herds do lie down, all herds, this is the first sad thing you learn — they
left us their bones to stand on and their last running still going round and
round the world. The windlanes are hoofprints, child. We live in the
hoofprints of the dead.

**Why we die.** Because we are *small*, and small things are quick, and quick
is paid for. The great ones lived ten thousand years and turned slow as
seasons. We get sixty, and we turn inside a wingspan. Fair trade. When you
die the Ossuin carry you down — let them, pay them, don't haggle — because a
body is ballast and a soul is lift, and what's left of you wants to go *down*
to where the new winds are made, the way rain goes down to come back as
weather. Anyone who tells you the dead are gone has never flown a lane their
grandmother charted.

**Why we are here.** To *carry*, little wing. Word, cargo, kin, fire,
medicine — the world is alive exactly as long as things move through it.
Still air is dead air; a still person is a dead person with the paperwork
pending. You were not born to hold ground. There is no ground.

**The others.** The Roak? Respect them, charge them double. They think the
wind is writing and want to read it; the wind is *running* and wants to be
run with — but a Roak will mend your glider truer than your own sister, and
their word on paper holds when wingbeats fail. The Ossuin — hush, lower your
voice the way I am doing. We owe them. Don't ask what. When a priest of the
Stair asks you for passage, you fly them free, you fly them first, and you
do not ask them questions over open sky. The Crown-folk and their Spirarchy
are landlords of a thing nobody can own; smile, pay the toll, remember the
lane was ours before their grandfathers learned to count it. And the Hushed —
my mother stopped the rope-work when I asked, the only time she stopped —
*the Hushed are what happens when the world forgets to keep moving. Pity
them on paper. Flee them in person.*

## What My Father Told Me (a Roak childhood, Suruveil's Crown)

My father told me this in the gallery stacks, in his copying-voice, which is
quieter than his praying-voice and far more serious.

**Where the world came from.** We do not know, and that sentence, said
honestly, is the founding stone of everything your lineage has built. What we
*observe*: the world is a sphere of air around a deep we cannot sound; the
Spires are biological husks of a vanished megafauna; the wind exhibits
structure — grammar — that mere heating of air should not produce. The vulgar
say the wind is alive. The careful say the wind is *patterned*, and that
every pattern has an author, and that the author is not in evidence. Our
whole kindred is one long attempt to read a book whose first pages are
missing. — Yes. Missing. You will learn, when you are older and bonded to
the archive, that the missing pages are not a metaphor. Sit with that
discomfort. It is the family inheritance.

**Why we die.** Entropy, formally. The body is an archive of fire and the
fire un-copies itself. But hear the consolation of our craft: *nothing
recorded is wholly dead.* You will die; your hand will survive in the margins
of forty documents; your observations will outlive your observatory. The
Ossuin take the body down and we do not interfere, because their rite is
older than our skepticism and — note this, it is the kind of fact that
matters — *their rite has never once changed.* Unchanging testimony is the
most precious document there is.

**Why we are here.** To complete the Record. Not to rule it — leave that
appetite to the Spirarchy — to *complete* it. Every debt witnessed, every
star charted, every windlane's schedule taken down twice by independent
hands. The world is winding down, child; even the lamp-burn tables say so,
though we do not say it loudly. If it winds down to nothing, the Record is
the proof we were here. If it can be wound back up — then somewhere in the
Record is the instruction, and someone of ours had better be able to find it.

**The others.** The Vael are the Record's bloodstream; never let contempt
for their bookkeeping become contempt for their knowledge — a Lanemother
holds in memory what would take us nine volumes, error rate enviable. The
Ossuin are our opposite number: they keep one document, orally, perfectly,
and will not let us copy it. The day the Deepway opens its chant-vault to the
Quillate is the day the world's two halves of the truth meet — your
great-grandmother petitioned for it annually until they stopped returning her
letters. The Spirarchy: we staff it, we draft its laws, and we privately
know the Settling Right is a citation to a document in a sealed room. Serve
it anyway; order is the climate scholarship needs. And the Hushed — they are
a *data point*, the most important one in nine hundred years. A person
subtracted from the wind. Whatever was subtracted is what the wind *is*.
Someone must interview them properly before the soldiers and the cultists
spoil the sample. It will probably have to be one of us.

## What My Priest Told Me (an Ossuin childhood, Carrowspar)

The Stairwarden told me this on the Long Stair itself, on my seventh
descent, where the lamplight goes red and you can hear the Undermist turn
over below you like a sleeper.

**Where the world came from.** Breathe out. Now wait — feel that need, the
little death before you breathe in? Hold the feeling. That is the oldest
fact. The world is a breath that something breathed *out*, fledgling, and
all of us — wing, bone, lamp, song — live inside that exhalation. The great
ones breathed the world; the world is the shape of their generosity. We
nest in their bones. We light our lamps with their marrow. Say it plainly,
because our whole kindred's office is to say plain things slowly: *we live
inside the dead, on the goodness of the dead, and we have not said thank
you properly in a thousand years.*

**Why we die.** To pay. Death is not punishment and not accident; it is the
installment. Every living thing borrows breath at birth and returns it at
death, and the place of returning is below, where the mist keeps the
world's accounts. This is why we carry the dead down — every kindred's dead,
the beggar and the Spirarch at the same shoulder-height — and why the chant
says what it says. You will sing the line ten thousand times in your life:
*we return what was taken; we do not return enough.* When you are older you
will notice the line does not say *what was given*. Notice it quietly.

**Why we are here.** To keep the door. Between the living sky and the deep
that renews it there must be a kindred that goes down with clean hands and
comes back with shut mouths, or the door becomes a market — imagine
Marrowers in the Undermist; you will need no other image of sin. We are
heavy, slow, long-lived, hard to frighten: built for the office. Mind the
door, mind the debt, mind the dead. Everything else — the pearls, the
politics, the Synod's nine chairs — is scaffolding around those three
duties.

**The others.** Love them; they are why the door matters. The Vael, who pay
their dead down promptly and grieve in motion — when a Moult raft drops
tether and circles Carrowspar in silence, that is mourning done correctly.
The Roak, who want to *copy the chant* — refuse them gently, forever; some
documents are true only in a living mouth. The Crown, which believes it
rules us because we have never corrected it. And the Hushed. Fledgling, the
others fear the Hushed wrongly. They are not the debt collected. They are
the debt *defaulted* — what remains when the world can no longer pay its
side. The Synod stopped the diving because of what the last diver heard
below. I will not tell you what she heard. I will tell you the Synod did
not stop out of fear. We stopped the way a family stops spending when it
finally reads the ledger.

## What My Chief Told Me (a Spindles childhood, the roostless)

Chief Ammet Threadbare told me this on the gleaning nets under Spindle
Seven, because that is where the children of nobody get their answers.

**Where the world came from.** Who knows, cub? The Vael say a herd, the
Roak say a book, the Ossuin say a breath, and every one of those stories
ends with *their* kindred holding something and ours holding the net. Here
is what your chief knows for certain: the world came from somewhere, and by
the time it got to us it was *secondhand*. We live in the spires nobody
wanted, fish the air nobody charted, burn the lamp-dregs. Whatever made the
world, it had heirs, and then it had us.

**Why we die.** Cold, mostly. Falls. Bad air in a stripped husk. The pretty
answers are for people who die in beds. But I'll give you the Spindles
answer too, the real one: we die *unrecorded*, and that is the part to be
angry about. The Quillate writes down every Crown merchant's sneeze; when
Spindle Nine shed its skin-quarter in the storm of '88, the Record gave it
one line. So we keep our own book — the Thread-book, every name, every
fall, sewn in knots because paper rots. You'll learn the knots. That's your
letters, cub. Nobody is forgotten off *my* nets.

**Why we are here.** To catch what falls. That's the work and the creed
both. The high galleries drop things — cargo, chances, people — and the
Spindles catch them, mend them, and sell them back. You are here because
somebody's grandmother caught somebody's grandfather in a net, and you'll
stay alive by being the kind of person others would dive after. Loyalty's
the only lift we own. Hold your people the way bone holds breath.

**The others.** The Crown is weather: real, dangerous, not personal —
don't curse at it, just rig for it. The Moult will feed you and never let
you forget the feeding; fly with them a season, learn everything, come
home. The Quillate is a locked door with the truth behind it — every
Spindles cub dreams of robbing it; the smart ones grow up to get *hired*
into it, which is the better robbery. The Deepway is the one debt we pay
first — they carried our dead down free in the year of the storm and wrote
nothing in any ledger about it, and the Spindles do not forget that the way
the Record forgets us. The Marrowers will offer you wages, cub, good ones,
and some year when the nets are thin you'll take them — just count what
they pay you against what they're draining out from under everyone, and
come home before the arithmetic makes you one of them. And the Hushed?
We're the only folk who've fed them, you know. Left bread on the dead-line
at Greywake fringe. It was gone in the morning, and *something* had
arranged the crumbs in a circle. Make of that what you like. Your chief
makes of it: even the emptied are somebody's people, and nobody else was
leaving bread.
