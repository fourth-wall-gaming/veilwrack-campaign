Monochrome ink engraving, a 19th-century steel-plate book illustration in the manner of Gustave Doré: black ink on warm off-white rag paper, built entirely from fine burin lines, dense cross-hatching, and stipple, with dramatic high-contrast chiaroscuro. A single full-page plate.

Subject: a winged kestrel-kin warrior — a slim feathered humanoid woman with russet-barred wings swept back into a steep, plummeting dive — drops spear-first out of a dusk sky onto a great leathery sky-drake, the spear-point driving through the root of the drake's wing. The two figures fall together against open air. Far below them the western face of a colossal bone-spire (the hollowed husk of a long-dead sky-leviathan) descends into haze, hung with ring upon ring of tiny oil-lamps tended on bone walkways — a whole constellation of small flames in the gathering dark. A low thin band of daylight breaks at the horizon.

Mood: doomed grandeur and vertigo; a small fierce figure against an immense, indifferent sky. Deep velvety blacks; bright untouched paper for the lamp-glow and the break of sky.

No colour. No lettering or signature inside the image. No modern objects or machinery. The Alar are people with real wings — not bird-headed, not angels.
