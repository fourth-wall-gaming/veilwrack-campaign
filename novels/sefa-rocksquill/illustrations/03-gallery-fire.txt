Monochrome ink engraving, a 19th-century book-frontispiece plate in the manner of Gustave Doré: black ink on off-white rag paper, dense cross-hatched shadow against firelit highlights, violent chiaroscuro, built from engraved line and stipple only. A single full-page plate.

Subject: the interior of a vaulted chamber of bone deep beneath a spire, in chaos and flame. A winged kestrel-kin warrior drops from a high timber-and-bone scaffold in the dark vault, a heavy single-edged falchion leading her dive, down toward a soft-faced robed man below — his feathered wing newly severed, his hand bringing a spark-striker to a spreading slick of lamp-oil. Fire races across the floor among woven mats and squat oil-casks. Beyond, ordinary winged folk scramble toward a far stone archway where a single signal-flare burns like a low star. The only light is tallow lamps and the first bloom of fire, carving the figures out of deep blacks; smoke gathers in the vault above.

Mood: catastrophe and sacrifice; heat, smoke, and motion frozen at its peak.

No colour. No lettering inside the image. No modern machinery or firearms. The Alar are winged people, not angels.
