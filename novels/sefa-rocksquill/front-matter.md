There is no ground in this world. There is only the sky, and the wind that fills it, and the people who are born with wings to ride it. They are the Alar — feathered folk, human in the face and body, with a true pair of wings folded from the back. The kestrel-kin are slim and quick; the corvid-kin heavy and black; the condor-kin tall and pale. They do not remember a time before they flew, and they do not imagine a time after.

They live on the spires: enormous pale towers of weathered bone that stand alone in the open air, the husks of sky-leviathans dead so long that no one living has seen one move. The Alar hollowed out those bones and made homes in them, ring above ring, and hung the spires with thousands of small oil-lamps tended at dusk and dawn along the bone walkways. Between the spires run the lanes — roads of moving air, the living wind that carries everyone everywhere, that brings the trade and the news and the wandering shoals of small sky-creatures the people hunt. The wind is the sea, the road, and the breath of the world all at once.

And the wind is dying.

It is called the Stilling. In places the air simply stops — goes flat and dead and silent, with no breath in it at all and no lift for a wing. These still zones open like sores, out along the old lanes and under the spires, and season by season they spread, and no one knows how to close them. Worse than the dead air are the people it takes. An Alar caught too long in a Stilling goes *Hushed*: they fall silent, and then soundless, and then a stillness begins to come off them like cold off ice, and what is left walks and looks at you with nothing behind its eyes. No one knows how to bring them back, either.

Against all of this stand the Gale Wardens — rangers and rescue-fliers who chart the dying lanes, pull the living out of the dead air, and keep watch on the spreading quiet.

One of them is Sefa Rocksquill. She is Vael, kestrel-kin, but she was born too big and too heavy-feathered for a kestrel — built like something that has no business flying as well as she does — and she never fit the courier-rafts of the Moult, the raft-nation of her people. The Moult has lost lanes to the Stilling, and kin with them. So Sefa carries the hatred of the dead wind the way a sailor hates the calm that starves him, and she found the one place in the sky where being heavy is a virtue: the Wardens' lances, who fight in a single hard diving pass and end a thing before it can end them.

This is the record of one of her nights, at the spire called Lanternfall, where the lamps are lit against a dark that is only getting wider.
