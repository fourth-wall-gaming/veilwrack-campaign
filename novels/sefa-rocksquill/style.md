# Style Card: Gamesmaster

The voice of the table -- how the GM narrates live play, set down as prose.
Close, sensory, moment-to-moment. The reader sits where the player sat.

## Sentence rhythm
Built for momentum, beat by beat. Mostly medium sentences that move with the
action and the breath, broken by short hard ones at the hit, the shock, the
landing. Let a sentence run long when a scene holds its breath, then cut.
Paragraphs are beats: one perception, one action, one outcome. The rhythm
follows the body, not the grammar.

## Diction
Concrete and sensory first. Name what the character feels on her skin and in
her chest -- heat, weight, the lean of a flame, dead air going flat in the
lungs. Plain words and this world's nouns (wing, tallow, marrow, aneroid,
soot, oil). Evocative when it is physical, never ornamental. Numbers plain.
Abstractions live only in a character's mouth.

## Dialogue
The spine of every scene. Quote the table's actual lines verbatim and let the
voices carry -- villains explain themselves warmly, Wardens answer short and
dry. "said" or untagged. The real subject -- fear, grief, the dying wind --
stays under the words. Humor is allowed, and human.

## Description density
Immersive but economical. One vivid sensory anchor per beat, then move. The
air is the weather: whether the wind lives or is dead is felt in the body
every scene. Light and dark, altitude and the drop, breath and silence are
the camera. Put the reader inside the character's senses, not in the room
looking on.

## POV and tense
Close third on the Warden whose night it is, past tense, but written with the
immediacy of play -- as if narrated a beat ahead of the outcome. Stay in her
body and perception throughout. Render the stakes from inside: what she sees,
attempts, fears. No omniscience, no narrator who knows the ending.

## Signature moves
- Fiction first: open a beat with the situation as the character perceives it
  and why it is uncertain, then the action, then the outcome -- the order the
  GM calls it at the table.
- Play as lived action: a contested roll becomes a physical attempt that wins
  or fails on the page (the pull that will not come, the dive that opens on
  nothing), never odds or mechanics.
- Sensory tension: build dread through the body -- the flame leaning, the
  aneroid at zero, the held breath -- not through commentary.
- The human beat: let a dry line or a small kindness land before moving on.

## Never do
No meta about dice, luck, rolls, or mechanics. No omniscient asides about fate
or the world's doom. No GM-only lore -- plot from the journal, texture from
player-visible lore only. Do not pull back to a wide narrator lens: if the
character cannot sense it, it is not on the page. Never name the emotion
outright -- show it in breath, hands, the unsaid.
