# Chapter 2 — The Ring

<!-- events: myth-event-859ee079d68b, myth-event-e8233a49bd77 -->

He found her on the lowest ring before the dawn brief, in the hour when the lamps come down — when a tender walks the western face snuffing the night's wicks in order, highest to lowest, so that the light withdraws down the spire ahead of the day like a tide going home to the gold.

Tilve Wickmend worked without hurry and without waste, a small Roak woman with wax-scarred hands, and Vorrh stood at the rail of the ring-walk and watched her tend three lamps before he could make himself approach, because the Deepway trains its divers for every silence in the world except this one. At the lowest ring the lamps burned strangely — faintly blue at the wick-heart, a colour he had no entry for in any table he'd been taught — and she mended as she went, he saw: wicks another tender would have stripped and discarded, she trimmed, and coaxed, and kept.

She knew he was there. She did not turn. He understood, watching the line of her back, that people had been *at* her for two years — the concerned, the curious, the cruel — and that her stillness was a wall built one visitor at a time.

So he did not say any of the things the visitors would have said. He stood at the working distance a lamp-tender's courtesy allows, and said, in the descent-cadence, the speech-rhythm of Carrowspar that every Deepway household in the sky would know from its funerals:

"My name is Vorrh. I was stair-cohort to Hollm, named the held breath at the same first descent. I was on the tether the day he went below the skim, and the record they sealed of that day is false, and I have always known it was false, and I have come a long way to say so to the one person it was sealed against."

The wick-knife stopped.

She did not turn for a long moment, and when she did her face was not what he had braced for. Not grief, not the wild thing the roost-hall whispers implied. Attention. Total, fathomless attention, like the flat light off a still pocket, like the held gap in a chant — a listening so complete it had stopped leaving room for anything to be said back.

He took the bundle from against his breastbone and unwound the cold-wax in front of her, kneeling at the ring-walk rail, the way offerings are unwrapped at Carrowspar: deliberately, both hands shown. Inside lay the small waxed bone with its two written words. And riding the bone, where it had ridden two years through every port and every silence he had carried it: the ring. Spliced sinew, diver-made — Hollm's own splice, Vorrh knew the lay of his brother's cordwork the way one knows a voice — broken once, and mended, the mend turned inward, *toward her,* the way a maker mends a thing whose breaking he cannot forgive himself.

"He was coming back to you," Vorrh said. "Whatever called him below the skim, he went under wearing this with the mend turned toward Lanternfall. I found him at the reef-bone months after, one arm raised toward the surface. No one carried him down. No one witnessed but me, after. The Synod wrote *complete and sealed* over an empty rite, and they handed you the seal and called it a husband." His voice held the cadence; the cadence was the only thing holding it. "The bone is witnessed, and the carrying-down is mine to finish — that debt is the Deepway's and I am keeping it whether the Deepway will say my name or not. But the ring was never the dark's. It was mended toward you. I have carried it wrong for two years."

He held it out: the ring alone, freed of the wax.

Tilve Wickmend looked at the ring, and at him, and at the ring. Below them the skim turned over, gold on slow gold, with the patience of a thing that is owed everything and can wait. Her scarred hand came out and closed around the splice — not snatching, not trembling; *receiving,* with the exact economy of a tender taking a lamp into keeping — and she pressed her closed fist once against her sternum, where a word would have lived if she had any left.

Then she turned and went back to her wicks.

That was all. It was not nothing. He had been raised on liturgies and had just watched one, complete in every part, performed in one gesture by a silent woman with wax-scarred hands; and as he wound the cold-wax back around the bone alone, it occurred to Vorrh that of the two of them, she had just spoken and he had just listened, and the Synod could have learned something from either half of it.

Behind him as he climbed for the dawn brief, eighty-one lamps burned down the western face, and one tender moved among them, and the lowest wicks burned blue.

Esk was at her charts when he came back up, as if she had not moved in the night, and he kept the contract one minute ahead of his courage:

"First, and all of it," Vorrh said. "I came to Lanternfall for your lampwright. My stair-cohort brother was her betrothed. This morning I gave her his ring, and she kept it. And there is a thing I have to do at the skim, and I am asking your leave to do it."

He told her all of it — the false record, the bone, the carrying-down owed; that the survey contract would take him to the skim above the very root-line where Hollm went under; that an Ossuin debt that old does not wait for a convenient season. He asked for the rite.

Esk heard him out, arms folded, with the dead lanes at her back, and when he finished she was quiet for the space of three lamp-calls from below.

"Conditions," she said. "Three. The survey comes first — every measurement on the contract, complete, before you spend one breath on the dead; I am not sending a grieving man down a dying lane, I am sending a surveyor down with grief in his kit, and you will keep them in that order. Second: my clause, no exceptions, *especially* below — everything that happens at that skim comes back up to me, first and whole, whatever it is, however it sounds. Third: you dive on Warden tether discipline, full crew, drum and count, none of your order's lone-descent doctrine. If the dead are owed a rite, the living are owed a line."

"Agreed," Vorrh said. "All three."

"Then you have my leave." She turned to the gallery door and raised her voice exactly as much as it needed. "Kithrel."

The flier came in from the landing-walk with the dawn behind her — Vael, quick-eyed, lane-tokens braided into her primaries, where they clicked very faintly when she moved, like small bones agreeing with each other.

"Kithrel of the Moult. Best survey-flier in the draft; she's flown every Stilling edge since Greywake closer than regulations allow, and she keeps a count the way the Quillate keeps a ledger." Esk's eyes came back to Vorrh, and there was, for the first time, something underneath the measurement — not warmth; *placement*, a commander setting two pieces where they belonged on a board only she could see whole. "She holds your line and your count today. Brief her on all of it — the contract, the survey, and the rite. She holds the count for *everything* you do down there, diver. That's the condition under the conditions."

Kithrel looked him over once, survey-fashion, missing nothing — the bottles, the wax, the new grief worn old.

"Contract's the edge," she said. "Chart the pocket, sound the margins, come back. I'll learn the rest on the stage." And then, with the first and only softness anyone had spent on him since he made landfall: "I keep good count, Deepway. Nobody goes off my line."

The sun cleared the haze to the east, and the last of Tilve's lamps went out below them, right on schedule, and the three of them went down to the diving stage together.
