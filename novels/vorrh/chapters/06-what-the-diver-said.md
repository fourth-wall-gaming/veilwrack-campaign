# Chapter 6 — What the Diver Said

<!-- events: myth-event-a9e5ea7fc01d, myth-event-74d142c89b82 -->

Commander Esk Veil-Torn kept her chart-room in the old beacon-keeper's gallery on Lanternfall's western face, where the light came in late and brass-coloured through panes of scraped horn, and the walls were charts, and the charts were dying.

That was how Vorrh had thought of it since his first hour in the draft. Other commanders hung trophies. Esk hung the Greywake windlane, inked by some Moult chart-singer a decade gone, with its lane-shrines and its tally-knots and its margins annotated in three hands — and across it, in the Warden draft's flat red, the creeping season-lines of the Stilling that had swallowed it. Lane after lane along the gallery wall, each with its red tide-marks. A library of obituaries. She worked with her back to none of them. She had earned her deed-name going into the Greywake silence after her wing-sister, and had come out alone, torn, carrying the body anyway, and it was said in the draft that she had hung that chart where visitors would have to look at it so that she would never have to explain herself twice.

She heard his report standing, arms folded, and did not interrupt once. That was her way; she was economical, scrupulously fair, allergic to speeches, and she paid for measurements.

He gave her measurements.

"Your charts put the pocket's top at three hundred meters. It rides at two hundred and eighty. The chart isn't in error — the chart was right when it was inked, this season. The pocket has come up twenty meters since." He laid the survey slate on her table, turned for her reading. "Your charts put the skim at six hundred. It stands at four hundred. And the pocket's edge cannot be charted at all, by me or anyone, because it is not a border. It moves between fixes. It's a tide-line, Commander. We have all been drawing shores on maps and calling them walls."

Esk's eyes went along the slate line by line. "And the soundings?"

"Dead. All of them. The rites smear and fail — and they fail westward, every casting, together, toward the pocket." He paused, and then gave her the rest, because he had decided on the climb that she would have all of it. "It reaches, Commander. While I was at the skim it extruded a limb of dead air eight meters thick across two hundred meters of open sky. I flew into it on the ascent. When I cleared it and fixed its line, its axis bore exactly on the patch of skim where I was working. It reached toward a sustained sound, along a bearing, to a place. And when the sound ended, it withdrew — half a meter to the minute, smooth, like a man drawing back his hand from a table."

The chart-room was quiet. Below the horn panes, faint and ceaseless, the evening commerce of the spire went on: wings, pulley-squeal, a lamp-crew calling its ring-count down the western face.

"Toward a sustained sound," Esk said. "What sound?"

He could have said *survey casting.* The slate would have borne him out. But she had given him leave for the rite under three conditions and the second had no exceptions, *especially below,* and he had agreed with the dawn not an hour old; and besides, he found he did not want the door the lie held open. He had crossed a sky to get away from sealed records.

"The funeral," Vorrh said. "The rite you gave me leave for. I tried the descent-chant alone, by the book, twice, and the form broke both times, and I learned why: the cohort-rite cannot be performed by one voice — not poorly; not at all. Which is the last thing I will ever need to know about the Synod's record, because the record swears that rite was performed entire, by witnesses never named, for a body never found. Then I tried a third time, and when I held the cohort-gap open — the answering-rest, the gap a second voice fills — the silence held it with me. On the rest-length. Fluently. Like a voice that knew the liturgy. After that I gave the dark my own words, and the bone with them, and the limb withdrew, and your flier's count was waiting for me above the band, and that is the whole of it, in order, as agreed." His voice was still a rasp; it made the words sound older than they were. "First, and all of it."

Esk looked at him for a long moment, with the dying lanes of a decade hung at her back.

"That's why you fly for *me,*" she said, which from Esk Veil-Torn was a commendation with full honours. She turned the slate face-down, deliberately, the gesture of a woman closing one ledger and opening a worse one. Then she went back along his report the way she went along a chart, fix by fix, and he watched her arrive where he had been trying not to stand since the climb.

"Walk it again," she said. "The limb crossed your tether-line on the ascent — a line that wasn't there at your descent. Its axis bore on your rite-site. Your soundings died westward, *all of them, together,* from your first casting of the morning — before the rite, before the funeral, before you gave it one syllable of liturgy." Her arms came unfolded; she set both hands flat on the chart-table, and for the first time since he had known her the economy in her face looked like what it had always been, which was armour. "Diver, I have been charting the Stilling as weather for ten years. Weather doesn't take a bearing. That thing wasn't drifting across your morning. It was *following* it. It found your sound, and it tracked the sound to you, and when you stopped making it, it withdrew and went home — and it knows, now, where the sounds of this spire come up from. We didn't survey the dead air today." The brass light moved on the dead lanes behind her. "It surveyed *us.*"

The lamp was the loudest thing in the room for a while.

"And it will have heard more than my rite," Vorrh said. "The lamp-calls. The drum. The whole western face, every dawn and dusk, on schedule, to the minute."

"To the minute," Esk agreed, flat as the flat light he had hung in, and let the arithmetic of that sit between them. Then: "There's one more fix, and you've earned the bad half of this too. Tilve Wickmend stood her count last night, at the western root-line, on the watch's record like every night for two years. Eighty-one beats, the wardens will swear, never one more." A beat, measured, like a count of her own. "Last night it went to eighty-two."

"Last night," Vorrh said. *While I was below.* The floor of him had not come back since dawn; it went further away now. His brother's count had stopped at the wrong number and no one had finished it; he had sworn at the skim, to the gold, to whatever held caesuras in the deep, *I will finish it* — and the same night, on the face above his rite, a count two years frozen had moved one beat. The sky had its own broken counts, and they were moving, and they were moving *together.*

"First change in two years, the same night an Ossuin diver holds a funeral at the skim and the silence answers it back," Esk said. "I command a survey draft, Vorrh. I am paid to mistrust coincidence, and I find I cannot afford this one."

She did not say: *and this morning you put a ring in that woman's hand.* She did not have to. It was in the room with them, in her clause and his agreement, first and all of it, both directions.

"Kithrel talked my drum-master out of hauling you up through the middle of that limb — on her own authority, which she did not have, and which I have just given her retroactively because she was right. She's offered to stand the night watches on the western face. Unpaid." A dry flicker. "The Moult does not offer unpaid anything, so you might consider what she thinks she's watching over."

"Commander," Vorrh said, "what is your question?"

Because there was one. He had learned her that well at least: Esk Veil-Torn did not deal in revelations, she dealt in decisions, and she had not turned that slate face-down to share his awe.

She straightened from the chart-table. Behind her the dead lanes hung in their red tide-marks, and below the horn panes the lamp-crews called their counts, and somewhere down the western face a silent woman was lighting the evening rings on schedule, eighty-two beats into a count whose ending no one living knew.

"The count moved when you gave the silence what it wanted," Esk said. "So you tell me, diver — you that it answered. The lamp-crews say the full diver's count runs to one hundred and eight. What happens when she gets there?" The brass light through the horn was going out of the room by degrees, the way it went out of everything. "And do I hope for it — or do I post wardens to stop it?"

The lamps came on, ring below ring below ring, down into the dark.
