# Chapter 1 — The Commander's Terms

<!-- events: myth-event-d6ac63bef692 -->

He came to Lanternfall Spire in the season of the High Gales, in the thousand-and-first year of a Record he no longer entirely believed, with breath-bottles, cold-wax, a name the Deepway would not say aloud, and a debt that belonged to a dead man.

The spire rose out of the brass haze like the rib of something that had once been too large for the sky to refuse — which was, of course, exactly what it was. Three thousand souls roosted on the bones of the dead leviathan, most of them Roak lamp-crafters, and their lamps climbed its western face in great descending rings, orange above, deepening toward gold below, down and down toward the Undermist, so that in the last light the spire seemed a thing already half-swallowed, wearing its own drowning as jewelry. Its windlane had begun to die that season. Vorrh had heard it said in three ports before he came. Nobody flies *toward* a Stilling.

He did. He had his reasons, and he told them to no one, and the first person on Lanternfall to get any of them out of him was the commander of the Warden draft, because she asked.

Esk Veil-Torn received him in the old beacon-keeper's gallery, standing, with a decade of dead windlanes charted on the walls behind her in flat red tide-marks. She was Vael, scarred along the jaw and the left wing-root, and she read his papers — diver's certification from Carrowspar, the only legal Undermist training in the sky; no current Deepway standing; the gap in the record where his standing used to be — the way she presumably read everything, once, completely, without comment.

"An Ossuin death-diver with no order," she said. "Cast out for what?"

"Asking a question twice."

"What question?"

"How a funeral record could be complete when I was there and it wasn't."

Esk looked at him for a moment over the top of the papers. Whatever the look cost her, she paid it without expression. "I command survey on a dying lane, diver. I lose tools, I lose charts, and some seasons I lose fliers, and I have read every record the Wardens hold on every one of them, and I will tell you what I have learned about complete records." She set the papers down. "There is no such thing. There is only what came back up the tether. You'll do."

---

The terms took less time than the papers had.

"One condition, and it is not negotiable, and it is the same one I give everyone." She came around the chart-table and stood in the late brass light from the horn panes, arms folded. "I am told first. About everything. Before your conscience, before your gods, before whatever order threw you out and whatever you still owe it. Whatever you find down there — whatever you see, whatever you break, whatever you bring up that you wish you hadn't — I hear it first, and I hear all of it. I don't pay for heroics and I don't forgive surprises. The Stilling gives me enough of those."

"And in exchange?"

"In exchange I tell you the truth back, which you will discover is not a common arrangement on this spire, or any other." She said it without bitterness, as a measurement. "Honest accounting, both directions. That's the whole contract. Everything else is rates."

Vorrh had stood in the Synod's halls and been ordered to unhear a singing. He had carried a question through two askings and an exile because no one in the Deepway would deal with him plainly. He looked at this torn, economical woman with her wall of dead lanes, offering him truth-for-truth as if it were a standard clause, and he thought: *I have flown a long way to find the thing I was thrown out for asking, and it is a hiring term in a doomed survey draft.*

"Agreed," he said. "First, and all of it."

"Then you're mine. Welcome to the dying lane." She turned back to the table, and he thought the audience was over, and he was wrong. Her voice changed — not softened; *narrowed*, the way a lamp narrows to a beam. "There's one more thing you'll want before you go below. Not an order. A briefing. You're Ossuin; your people keep the descents and the dead. So you should know what this spire has already lost down there, because you'll be working over the top of it."

---

"Tilve Wickmend," Esk said. "Lamp-tender, western face — the rings you'll dive past every morning of your contract. Nine years on the lamps. Two years ago she went below the skim on a tether, past the root-line, and came up alive." A beat. "She hasn't spoken since. Not a word, not a written one. The crews call her the hushed lampwright, mostly when they think she can't hear them, which is always. Which is the problem."

The brass light moved on the dying charts.

"She still lights every lamp," Esk said. "Every ring, every night, to the minute, nine years of schedule and the silence hasn't taken one wick from her. And every night, when the lowest ring is lit, she stands at the western root-line and counts. The diver's count — you'd know it better than I would. Eighty-one beats." Esk's arms stayed folded. "And stops. Eighty-one, and stops. Every night, two years, never one beat more, and the watch-wardens will swear it has never varied. Whatever she went down to find, diver, she is still counting toward it, and the count does not finish."

"What was she diving for?" Vorrh asked. His voice came out level, which was a craftsman's piece of work, because the floor of him had gone out somewhere around *the western root-line.* A lamp-tender of Lanternfall. On a tether, past the root-line, two years ago. He carried, wrapped in cold-wax against his breastbone, a ring spliced of sinew, broken once, mended toward a lamp-tender of Lanternfall — and he had crossed the sky to find her, and had not yet so much as asked her name in the roost-halls, and now it was given to him unasked, by a commander honoring a contract one minute old. *Told first. Both directions.*

"For a body," Esk said. "Her betrothed went under at the skim on a Deepway dive two years back. The Synod's record says he was carried down — complete, sealed, all the right words. She went down anyway. Make of that what you like." She looked at him, and there was nothing in her face but the measurement, and the measurement was thorough. "You asked your order how a record could be complete when you were there and it wasn't. So I'll tell you what the western face thinks of complete records, diver: there's a woman down on the lowest ring who has been silent for two years, counting to eighty-one, and the record says she has nothing left to wait for."

Vorrh said nothing. The ring lay against his breastbone like a coal.

"Survey brief is at the stage at dawn," Esk said, returning to her charts. "Go settle in. And Vorrh —" she did not look up — "whatever it is you flew here carrying: first, and all of it. That clause has no exceptions, and you have until dawn."
